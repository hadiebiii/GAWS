Project Summary

Overview

As next-generation wireless communications, ubiquitous remote sensing, and quantum-computing architectures scale toward unprecedented dimensionalities, they encounter a fundamental and shared physical bottleneck: the necessity of routing highly fragile, multi-dimensional physical modes through a severely constrained number of imperfect hardware interfaces. In millimeter-wave (mmWave) and sub-terahertz (sub-THz) phased arrays, massive spatial degrees of freedom must be compressed to share a limited pool of radio-frequency (RF) chains, local-oscillator (LO) distribution networks, and analog-to-digital converters (ADCs). Concurrently, in cryogenic quantum computing, hundreds of highly sensitive control and readout signals are funneled through a scarce number of low-temperature coaxial interconnects and power-limited cryogenic amplifiers.

Classical multiplexing paradigms isolate these channels mathematically via Code-Division (CDM), Time-Division (TDM), or Frequency-Division (FDM) multiplexing. However, these methods presume idealized abstract orthogonality. In physical implementations, hardware non-idealities—including sub-picosecond timing skew, phase quantization, non-linear distortion, package dispersion, and thermal drift—inevitably destroy this orthogonality. This degradation precipitates catastrophic inter-channel crosstalk, exhausts ADC dynamic range, and severely bounds system scalability.

This CAREER project pioneers Geometry-Aware Waveform Synthesis (GAWS), a transformative circuit-signal co-design methodology. Instead of applying idealized mathematical codes and relying on power-hungry digital calibration backends to correct physical errors, GAWS treats every spatial beam or quantum control pulse as a hardware-realized response signature. The explicit target of optimization is the realized hardware Gram matrix—the geometric representation of signal separation after physical propagation, non-linear distortion, and receiver projection.

The central hypothesis asserts that synthesizing finite-alphabet, circuit-realizable waveforms using complex Grassmannian and Spherical-Gold packing algorithms can constrain mutual coherence near theoretical minimum bounds, even under severe physical impairments. By preserving this geometric conditioning prior to digitization, GAWS fundamentally relaxes ADC effective number of bits (ENOB) requirements, dramatically mitigates crosstalk, and enhances system resilience to synchronization errors.

Intellectual Merit

The intellectual merit of this research is rooted in a paradigm shift: abandoning the pursuit of abstract mathematical orthogonality in favor of directly optimizing the geometric separability of hardware-distorted signals. This theoretical advance will be empirically validated across two distinct, highly constrained technological platforms:

  • Platform A (Primary Validation): A hardware-in-the-loop mmWave (Ka-band) phased-array receiver. This testbed will utilize analog correlation pre-digitization to physically demonstrate reductions in data-converter dynamic-range burdens, unprecedented jammer suppression, and refined spatial Cramér-Rao bounds.
  • Platform B (Cross-Domain Emulation): An advanced microwave emulation architecture for cryogenic quantum interfaces. It will apply the same realized-Gram conditioning principles to frequency-division multiplexed (FDM) resonator readouts and selective XY-control pulses, demonstrating superior crosstalk suppression under strict photon and amplification constraints.

The technical execution is divided into four interdependent thrusts encompassing theoretical manifold optimization (Thrust 1), mixed-signal integrated circuit (IC) design (Thrust 2), and rigorous multi-platform experimental validations (Thrusts 3 and 4).

Broader Impacts

The broader impacts of this proposal are driven by an integrated educational and outreach framework designed to obliterate the persistent silos separating microwave engineering, digital signal processing, and quantum information science. Central to this effort is the creation of a novel graduate curriculum, "Realized-Gram Methods for RF and Quantum Interfaces," paired with hands-on, software-defined radio (SDR) undergraduate laboratories. Furthermore, the PI will develop and release an open-source, interactive simulation toolkit allowing students to visually comprehend how hardware limits degrade abstract math, and how geometry-aware synthesis restores system integrity. Outreach initiatives leverage these visual tools in university-sponsored engineering camps, explicitly targeting the recruitment and retention of underrepresented minority (URM) students into deep-tech hardware disciplines.

1. Introduction and Context

1.1 The Shared-Interface Bottleneck in Modern Architectures

As information systems aggressively scale, the interconnects bridging processing cores to physical environments have become the paramount architectural bottleneck. This limitation is starkly evident in two highly active, yet historically disconnected domains: wideband multi-beam phased arrays and scalable cryogenic quantum processors.

In mmWave and sub-THz phased arrays, scaling spatial resolution mandates a massive increase in antenna elements ($M$) and concurrent beams ($N$). While a fully digital architecture—allocating a dedicated high-speed ADC to every antenna—provides maximum capacity, its prohibitive thermal dissipation, physical footprint, and power demands render it unviable for mobile edge devices and satellite swarms. Consequently, these systems are forced into hybrid architectures, multiplexing multiple physical modes through a compressed set of analog-to-digital boundaries.

Similarly, superconducting quantum processors operating inside dilution refrigerators face strict thermodynamic boundaries. With cooling power limited to tens of microwatts at the $20\,\text{mK}$ stage, routing individual control lines for thousands of qubits is physically impossible. Current architectures rely on multiplexing multiple readout tones onto single coaxial lines. However, as tone density increases, the non-linear behavior of cryogenic components (e.g., Josephson Traveling-Wave Parametric Amplifiers) introduces severe intermodulation distortion and spectral leakage, directly inducing measurement errors and computational decoherence.

1.2 The Collapse of Abstract Mathematical Orthogonality

Conventional engineering attempts to solve these bottlenecks utilizing classical multiplexing. Code-Division Multiplexing (CDM) assigns mathematically orthogonal sequences (e.g., Walsh-Hadamard codes) to independent channels, assuming a zero inner product:

$$\langle c_i, c_j \rangle = 0 \quad (\forall i \neq j)$$

However, physical circuitry does not transmit abstract mathematical entities. Waveforms are subjected to an inescapable hardware impairment operator $\mathcal{D}_\theta$, parameterized by physical non-idealities $\theta$:

$$\widetilde{c}_k = \mathcal{D}_\theta(c_k)$$

When subjected to timing skew, finite slew rates, local oscillator jitter, and third-order nonlinearities, the realized physical waveforms rapidly lose their orthogonality. This collapse decimates the Signal-to-Interference-plus-Noise Ratio (SINR), saturates ADC dynamic ranges, and generates uncalibratable deterministic crosstalk.

1.3 The Geometry-Aware Solution

This project confronts the interface bottleneck by pivoting the design focus entirely onto the hardware-realized Gram matrix. Rather than designing perfect codebooks and relying on post-digitization DSP to salvage corrupted signals, we synthesize waveforms natively aware of physical circuit constraints. By modeling spatial angles, cryogenic tones, and temporal chips as vectors packed upon a complex Grassmannian manifold, we achieve a mechanism—herein termed Spherical-Gold Coding—that mathematically guarantees bounded and predictable crosstalk, pre-digitization.

2. Research Questions & Hypotheses

2.1 Formulating the Scientific Inquiry

The trajectory of this research is governed by the following core inquiries:

Central Scientific Question

How can finite-alphabet, circuit-constrained waveforms be synthesized such that their realized, impaired, and whitened response signatures maintain optimal geometric separation despite sub-picosecond timing skew, phase quantization, non-linear intermodulation, and environmental drift?

2.2 The Mathematical Core Hypothesis

Consider a system where $N$ independent channels share a constrained interface. Each channel $k$ is theoretically assigned an ideal waveform $\phi_k \in \mathbb{C}^d$, where $d$ represents available synthesis dimensions (e.g., temporal chips, spatial phases, spectral bins).

In hardware, the transmitted signature is distorted by $\mathcal{D}_\theta$, yielding $\widetilde{\phi}_k = \mathcal{D}_\theta(\phi_k)$. The parameter space $\theta$ encapsulates the primary hardware non-idealities:

$$\theta = \left\{ \Delta \tau, \, b, \, \tau_r, \, a_3, \, \epsilon_\phi, \, \epsilon_g, \, H_{\mathrm{pkg}}, \, \sigma_j \right\}$$

Table 1: Physical Impairment Parameters in the GAWS Model

Parameter Physical Definition Typical Units / Scaling
$\Delta \tau$ Inter-channel timing skew Picoseconds ($\text{ps}$)
$b$ Phase-quantizer DAC resolution $2$ to $6$ bits
$\tau_r$ Switching rise/fall time (slew rate) Volts/nanosecond ($\text{V/ns}$)
$a_3$ Third-order non-linear coefficient $\text{V}^{-2}$ (governs $\text{IIP3}$)
$\epsilon_\phi, \epsilon_g$ Residual phase and static gain mismatch Degrees ($^\circ$) / Decibels ($\text{dB}$)
$H_{\mathrm{pkg}}(f)$ Package/bondwire transfer function S-parameters ($\text{S}_{21}$, $\text{S}_{11}$)

Let $R \in \mathbb{C}^{d \times d}$ represent the noise-plus-interference covariance matrix. The whitened, normalized realized signature vector for channel $k$ is $u_k = (R^{-1/2}\widetilde{\phi}_k) / \| R^{-1/2}\widetilde{\phi}_k \|_2$. Collecting these vectors constructs the Hardware-Realized Gram Matrix ($G = U^H U$). The maximum off-diagonal magnitude defines the system's mutual coherence ($\mu_{\mathrm{real}}$).

Core Hypothesis

Finite-alphabet, circuit-realizable waveforms can be synthesized such that the realized mutual coherence ($\mu_{\mathrm{real}}$) remains asymptotically close to the theoretical Grassmannian (Welch) bound under severe physical impairments. This geometric conditioning directly minimizes inter-channel crosstalk, averts ill-conditioning in digital recovery algorithms (ZF/MMSE), and drastically reduces the dynamic-range resolution (ENOB) required of the digitizer.

3.1 Reviewer-Safe Scope Assurances

To fortify the scientific rigor and feasibility of this proposal, the scope is deliberately bounded by the following pragmatic truths:

  • Rejection of "Zero-Crosstalk" Fallacies: Geometry-aware design does not invoke magical zero-crosstalk scenarios. It guarantees bounded, mathematically predictable coherence under stress, transitioning crosstalk from an erratic failure mechanism into a calibratable variable.
  • Digital Supremacy Acknowledged: Given infinite power, area, and cooling capacity, fully digital DBF architectures remain the information-theoretic optimum. GAWS is championed exclusively for heavily constrained hardware envelopes where DBF fails.
  • RF Pre-Stage Realities: Analog correlation pre-ADC brilliantly shields the digitizer from saturation; however, it cannot retroactively un-compress an LNA driven into saturation by massive out-of-band blockers. Non-linear cascades remain physically limiting factors.

4. Mathematical Foundations & The Product Law

4.1 The Vulnerability of Temporal Orthogonality

Standard Code-Division systems assign a normalized temporal sequence $c_k \in \mathbb{C}^L$ to each channel. The ideal temporal Gram matrix $G_{\mathrm{code}} = C^H C$ equals the identity matrix $I_N$. However, under physical timing skew ($\Delta\tau$), the matrices desynchronize, yielding leakage from channel $j$ into $i$:

$$\text{Leakage}_{i \leftarrow j} = P_j \left| c_i^H c_j(\Delta\tau) \right|^2$$

This fractional-chip delay violently degrades power isolation ($I^{\mathrm{pow}}_{ij} = -10 \log_{10} |c_i^H c_j|^2$).

4.2 Spatial Phase Packing and the Spherical-Gold Mechanism

To arrest this collapse, GAWS synthesizes a joint signature. The temporal code is augmented by a physical-mode signature vector $w_k \in \mathbb{C}^M$ (e.g., across $M$ antenna ports). The synthesized joint signature becomes the tensor product $\phi_k = c_k \otimes w_k$. Utilizing tensor identities, the inner product—and thus the system coherence—factors beautifully:

$$\boxed{G_{\mathrm{eff}} = G_{\mathrm{code}} \circ G_{\mathrm{phys}}}$$

This Product Law of Mutual Coherence defines the Spherical-Gold mechanism. Converted to decibels, total isolation equals the sum of temporal and physical isolations:

$$I_{\mathrm{total,dB}}(\Delta\tau) = I_{\mathrm{code,dB}}(\Delta\tau) + I_{\mathrm{phys,dB}}$$

Consequently, if timing skew obliterates temporal isolation, the deliberately designed geometric physical isolation ($I_{\mathrm{phys,dB}}$) serves as a hard mathematical floor, preventing catastrophic system failure.

5. Capacity Limits & Dynamic Range Ceilings

The impact of the realized Gram matrix ($G_{\mathrm{real}}$) on multi-channel information capacity is governed by its eigenvalues $\lambda_\ell$. By the Gershgorin Circle Theorem, the matrix condition number is strictly bounded by the maximum mutual coherence $\mu_{\mathrm{real}}$:

$$\kappa(G_{\mathrm{real}}) \leq \frac{1 + (N-1)\mu_{\mathrm{real}}}{1 - (N-1)\mu_{\mathrm{real}}}$$

In high-SNR regimes ($\gamma \to \infty$), system performance does not scale indefinitely but strikes a physical dynamic-range ceiling dictated entirely by non-orthogonal leakage:

$$\text{DR}_{\text{ceiling,dB}} = -10 \log_{10} \left[ (N-1) \mu_{\mathrm{real}}^2 \right]$$

Applying the Spherical-Gold methodology reduces $\mu_{\mathrm{real}}$ multiplicatively, directly extending the linear dynamic range of the architecture by over $10\,\text{dB}$ before saturation occurs.

6. Resolving the ADC ENOB Bottleneck

The required ADC resolution (ENOB) must accommodate the target SNR, crest-factor headroom, and the aggregate power of all co-channel signals and out-of-band blockers ($B_{I}$). The essential scaling law is:

$$\text{ENOB}_{\text{req}} = \frac{1}{6.02} \left( \text{SNR}_{\text{req}} + M_{\text{head}} + 10 \log_{10} \left[ J_{\text{shared}} + \sum_{\ell=1}^{L_I} 10^{(B_{I,\ell} - A_{\mathrm{pre},\ell})/10} \right] - 1.76 \right)$$

GAWS introduces a powerful analog pre-suppression term ($A_{\mathrm{pre}}$) computed via analog cross-correlation prior to the quantizer. By suppressing massive blockers before they enter the ADC, we alleviate the ENOB requirement by $2$ to $3$ bits. Because ADC power consumption scales exponentially with resolution ($P_{\mathrm{ADC}} \propto f_s \cdot 2^{\mathrm{ENOB}}$), this technique generates a multiplicative reduction in overall receiver power dissipation.

7. Detailed Research Thrusts

Thrust 1: Theoretical Foundations of Realized-Gram Geometries

Objective:

To construct the definitive theoretical framework mapping finite-alphabet, hardware-constrained waveforms to their eventual realized Gram matrices ($G_{\mathrm{real}}$), specifically addressing non-separable physical impairments.

Methodology:

  • Impairment Sensitivity Analysis: Deriving closed-form bounds connecting the coherence perturbation $\Delta\mu$ directly to DAC phase quantization ($b$) and analog non-linearities ($a_3$).
  • Manifold Optimization Algorithms: Utilizing complex Grassmannian manifold projections to synthesize finite-state ($2\text{--}4\,\text{bits}$) optimal sequences bounded by strict Peak-to-Average Power Ratio (PAPR) limits.
Key Deliverables: Open-source mathematical formalism and a Monte-Carlo analysis toolkit mapping $\theta \to \mu_{\mathrm{real}}$.

Thrust 2: Circuit-Level Synthesis and Analog Projection

Objective:

To design, simulate, and tape-out the mixed-signal integrated circuit (IC) blocks capable of executing geometry-aware synthesis and pre-digitization correlation.

Methodology:

  • Ka-Band Waveform Synthesizer: Developing a high-speed polar-DAC structure ($26.5\text{--}40\,\text{GHz}$) featuring constant-impedance switching networks to suppress group delay variations.
  • Wideband Analog Correlator: Implementing a modified active Gilbert-cell multiplier to project the received analog vector onto the target signature space, establishing the spatial-code filtering prior to the ADC buffer.
Key Deliverables: Validated tape-out files, bench characterization of $\text{IIP3}$/NF, and demonstrated analog pre-suppression ratios.

Thrust 3: Platform A Validation (Phased-Array Multibeam)

Objective:

Empirical validation of GAWS methodologies within a physical, hardware-in-the-loop $28\,\text{GHz}$ multi-beam testbed.

Methodology:

Constructing a 4-antenna RF testbed to expose the system to concurrent target signals and hostile wideband jammers. The platform will systematically sweep parameters ($\Delta\tau, b$) to benchmark GAWS against conventional Walsh-CDM. Furthermore, the local spatial Cramér-Rao Bound (CRB) for Angle-of-Arrival (AoA) estimation will be physically measured to quantify spatial resolution improvements.

Key Deliverables: Measured dynamic-range relief mapping and comprehensive bit-error-rate (BER) sensitivity profiles under physical skew constraints.

Thrust 4: Platform B Translation (Cryogenic Qubit Interfaces)

Objective:

To securely translate phase-correlation methodologies from RF telecom space to the domain of superconducting quantum computing interfaces via high-fidelity hardware emulation.

Methodology:

Formulating an FDM resonator-bank emulator mimicking standard dispersive readout schemes. GAWS algorithms will optimize the reflection signature $S_{11}(\omega)$ and selective XY control pulses $p_k(t)$ to mitigate photon-starvation limits and Traveling-Wave Parametric Amplifier (TWPA) compression limits. The target is suppressing readout crosstalk $|G_{ij}^{\mathrm{ro}}|^2$ while preserving precision gate angles.

Key Deliverables: Emulator validation reports contrasting Grassmannian-optimized readouts versus standard FDM baseline fidelities.

9. Falsification Framework & Failure Modes

A hallmark of rigorous scientific inquiry is the active pursuit of failure modes. We have embedded a falsification framework dictating nine explicit failure conditions (FM1-FM9) that will be systematically tested. Critical modes include:

  • FM1: Non-Separable Interconnect Dispersion. Risk: The separability product law breaks down under extreme, frequency-dependent microstrip dispersion. Test: Injection of highly resonant passive networks into the testbed to measure product law degradation thresholds.
  • FM2: RF Pre-Stage Compression. Risk: Out-of-band blockers compress analog front-ends (LNA/Mixers) before the GAWS correlator can suppress them. Test: Empirical 1dB compression sweeps to chart the absolute physical boundary of the receiver cascade.
  • FM5: Thermal Calibration Drift. Risk: High thermal fluctuations degrade the geometric nulls over time. Test: Execution of $0^\circ\text{C}$ to $85^\circ\text{C}$ thermal chamber stress tests to quantify the temporal stability of $\mu_{\mathrm{real}}$.
  • FM7: Qubit Photon Starvation. Risk: Imposing strict spectral boundaries reduces readout power to levels that starve the cavity of necessary photons. Test: Evaluation of assignment fidelity at $\bar{n} < 1$ photon constraints.

11. Integrated Educational Plan & Broader Impacts

True to the ethos of the NSF CAREER program, the PI is deeply committed to synthesizing this deep-tech research into an actionable and highly accessible educational paradigm.

  • 11.1 Curriculum Innovation: The PI will introduce a pioneering graduate module, Realized-Gram Methods for RF and Quantum Interfaces. This course actively disassembles the historical divide between electromagnetic physics and digital signal processing, teaching students to co-design across these boundaries.
  • 11.2 Experiential Undergraduate SDR Labs: We will inject practical software-defined radio (SDR) modules into the undergraduate core. Students will visually experience how timing skews destroy classical code multiplexing and implement entry-level phase-coding to physically restore signal integrity.
  • 11.3 Open-Source Visual Sandbox: Expanding upon the simulator embedded within this very proposal document, we will deploy a comprehensive, web-based visualization tool. This allows learners to manipulate hardware variables (jitter, non-linearity) and instantly visualize the geometric collapse or preservation of the resulting Gram matrices.
  • 11.4 Strategic URM Outreach: Leveraging the visual tools and physical acoustic analogies, the PI will conduct immersive workshops within university-sponsored high school engineering camps. A dedicated portion of the budget is reserved for Undergraduate Research Fellowships explicitly targeting the recruitment of female and underrepresented minority students into high-frequency hardware research pathways.

12. Concluding Remarks

This CAREER proposal outlines a rigorously defined, physically grounded methodology to overcome the shared-interface bottleneck paralyzing next-generation wireless and quantum architectures. By elevating the hardware-realized Gram matrix to the primary metric of design, Geometry-Aware Waveform Synthesis shatters the brittle assumptions of classical ideal orthogonality.

Supported by an unyielding falsification framework, robust multi-platform validation strategies, and a deeply integrated educational vision, this project will furnish the scientific community with both the theoretical foundation and the tangible circuit architectures necessary to scale the complex communication and computational networks of the coming decade.
