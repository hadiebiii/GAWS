Project Summary

Overview:

Next-generation wireless communication, remote sensing, and cryogenic quantum-computing systems face a shared, fundamental circuit-to-system bottleneck: many fragile physical modes must pass through far fewer, imperfect physical interfaces. In millimetre-wave (mmWave) and sub-terahertz (sub-THz) phased arrays, dozens of antenna elements, beam directions, or spatial modes must share a limited number of radio-frequency (RF) chains, intermediate-frequency (IF) routing wires, local-oscillator (LO) distribution networks, and analog-to-digital converters (ADCs). In cryogenic quantum systems, multiple control and readout lines must share scarce, low-temperature coaxial interconnects, cryogenic low-noise amplifiers, microwave resonator banks, and power-limited room-temperature electronics.

Conventional multiplexing strategies divide these channels cleanly in time (TDM), frequency (FDM), or via mathematically ideal orthogonal codes (CDM). However, physical hardware destroys ideal mathematical separation through timing skew, finite rise and fall times, phase quantization, phase noise, package dispersion, non-linear distortion, and thermal drift.

This CAREER project establishes geometry-aware waveform synthesis, a transformative circuit-signal co-design framework in which each spatial beam, readout tone, or qubit control pulse is treated not as an ideal mathematical function, but as a hardware-realized response signature. The central object of optimization is not the ideal codebook, but the realized hardware Gram matrix after waveform synthesis, physical propagation, non-linear distortion, channel whitening, and receiver-kernel projection.

The primary objective is to keep this realized Gram matrix well-conditioned—maintaining large diagonal entries for robust signal recovery and minimizing off-diagonal entries to ensure bounded, measurable, and calibratable crosstalk. The project’s central hypothesis is that finite-alphabet, circuit-realizable Grassmannian and Spherical-Gold waveform synthesis can keep the realized mutual coherence near its fundamental theoretical bounds under real circuit constraints. This reduction in mutual coherence relaxes the dynamic-range burden on the ADC, minimizes calibration overhead, and reduces synchronization sensitivity.

Intellectual Merit:

The intellectual merit of this research lies in shifting the paradigm of shared-interface communications from "ideal orthogonality under hardware correction" to "direct optimization of hardware-distorted geometries." The research will be validated across two distinct, challenging hardware platforms:

  • Platform A (Primary Validation): A hardware-in-the-loop mmWave / Ka-band phased-array shared-interface receiver utilizing analog correlation before digitization to physically demonstrate data-converter dynamic-range relief, high jammer suppression, and improved angular resolution.
  • Platform B (Cross-Domain Translation): An interface-level emulation platform for cryogenic quantum interfaces, applying the same phase-correlation and Gram-conditioning principles to frequency-division multiplexed (FDM) resonator-bank readouts and selective XY-control pulse shaping. This platform evaluates assignment fidelity and crosstalk reduction under tight photon budgets and amplifier saturation constraints without claiming the direct operation of active multi-qubit chips.

The technical framework is built on four core research thrusts: Thrust 1 (Theory): Shifting from abstract Grassmannian packings to realized-Gram geometry under non-linear and time-varying hardware impairments. Thrust 2 (Circuits): Designing finite-alphabet waveform synthesis blocks and analog correlators that perform spatial-code projection before the ADC bottleneck. Thrust 3 (Platform A): Validating mmWave array operations, tracking beam separation, and quantifying spatial Cramer-Rao bound improvements. Thrust 4 (Platform B): Emulating multi-channel readout and control signatures for quantum interfaces under strict cryogenic constraints.

Broader Impacts:

The broader impacts of this CAREER project are anchored by an integrated educational and outreach plan designed to bridge the growing gap between physical microwave engineering, digital signal processing, and quantum information science. The educational plan includes: the development of a new graduate-level module: Realized-Gram Methods for RF and Quantum Interfaces; a hands-on undergraduate laboratory sequence centered on code-division multiplexed hardware; and the creation of an open-source, interactive simulation package, allowing students and engineers to visualize how hardware impairments degrade codebooks and how geometry-aware synthesis restores separability. Outreach activities aim at high school and underrepresented minority students through existing university-sponsored engineering camps, demonstrating the physics of interference and coding through visual and acoustic wave experiments.

1. Introduction and Project Overview

1.1 The Shared-Interface Bottleneck in Modern RF and Quantum Systems

As physical systems scale in complexity, the interconnects linking their processing cores to the external world emerge as primary design bottlenecks. This bottleneck manifests clearly in two highly active but historically isolated fields: wideband multi-beam phased arrays and scalable cryogenic quantum computing processors.

In mmWave and sub-THz phased-array systems, increasing the spatial resolution requires scaling the number of antenna elements ($M$) and synthesized concurrent beams ($N$). In an unconstrained scenario, a fully digital architecture—allocating a dedicated RF chain and a high-speed, high-resolution ADC to every element—provides the maximum information-theoretic capacity. However, the power consumption, thermal dissipation, cost, and physical space requirements of hundreds of wideband ADCs make fully digital arrays impractical for edge devices, automotive radars, and satellite communication terminals. To survive, these systems utilize hybrid architectures or analog beamforming, forcing multiple physical modes to share a reduced number of intermediate-frequency (IF) paths and ADCs.

Similarly, superconducting and spin-based quantum processors operating at millikelvin temperatures within dilution refrigerators face a strict thermal and physical wiring bottleneck. To avoid overwhelming the cooling power of the cryogenic refrigerator’s lowest stages (typically limited to $10\text{--}100\,\mu\text{W}$ at $20\,\text{mK}$), hundreds of control and readout lines must be multiplexed onto a minimal set of cryogenic coaxial cables. Conventional approaches utilize Frequency-Division Multiplexing (FDM) with passive superconducting resonator banks. However, as the density of readout tones increases, the non-linearities of cryogenic components (such as Josephson Traveling-Wave Parametric Amplifiers, or TWPAs) and the limited bandwidth of cryogenic low-noise amplifiers introduce intermodulation distortion, spectral leakage, and phase noise, causing severe readout crosstalk and gate errors.

1.2 The Failure of Abstract Orthogonality

To address these shared-interface problems, engineers traditionally turn to classical multiplexing techniques. Code-Division Multiplexing (CDM), for example, assigns each channel an orthogonal sequence (such as Walsh-Hadamard or Gold codes). Mathematically, these codes maintain a zero inner product:

$$\langle c_i, c_j \rangle = 0 \quad (\forall i \neq j)$$

However, real-world hardware does not transmit or receive abstract mathematical vectors. Instead, waveforms are subject to physical impairments represented by the operator $\mathcal{D}_\theta$, parametrized by hardware non-idealities $\theta$:

$$\widetilde{c}_k = \mathcal{D}_\theta(c_k)$$

Where $\theta$ encompasses timing skew, phase-quantizer resolution, finite edge speed, non-linear intermodulation, package dispersion, phase/gain mismatch, and clock jitter. Under these impairments, the realized waveforms lose their orthogonality, collapsing the system’s effective signal-to-interference-plus-noise ratio (SINR), saturating the dynamic range of subsequent ADCs, and creating uncalibratable crosstalk.

1.3 Proposed Solution: Geometry-Aware Waveform Synthesis

This project addresses the interface bottleneck by making the hardware-realized Gram matrix the explicit, primary object of co-design. Rather than designing an ideal codebook and appending complex, power-hungry digital calibration backends to fix hardware non-idealities, we synthesize waveforms directly within the constraints of the physical circuitry to minimize the mutual coherence of the actual, impaired waveforms.

We treat the physical modes, spatial angles, or cryogenic tones as vectors packed on a complex Grassmannian manifold. By co-designing the waveform's code properties (temporal) and circuit excitation paths (spatial, phase, and spectral), we achieve a product-domain leakage-suppression mechanism—termed Spherical-Gold Coding—that guarantees bounded, predictable, and calibratable crosstalk even under severe timing skew, phase quantization, and non-linearities.

2. Central Research Question and Core Hypothesis

2.1 Central Research Question

The scientific inquiry driving this research is formulated as follows:

Central Scientific Question

How can finite-alphabet circuit waveforms be synthesized so that their realized, impaired, and whitened response signatures remain well separated after timing skew, phase quantization, finite edge speed, non-linear distortion, package dispersion, and calibration drift?

Equivalent Circuit Formulation

Can a shared RF/IF/ADC or cryogenic interface be designed by directly minimizing the off-diagonal coherence of the hardware-realized Gram matrix, rather than by assuming ideal orthogonal codes?

2.2 Core Hypothesis

Let $N$ independent channels share a single physical interface. Each channel $k \in \{1, \ldots, N\}$ is assigned an ideal waveform signature:

$$\phi_k \in \mathbb{C}^d$$

where $d$ represents the available synthesis degrees of freedom (e.g., temporal chips, frequency tones, delay taps, spatial modes, spectral bins, or pulse-shaping coefficients).

In physical hardware, the actual signature generated and propagated through the interface is distorted by the hardware impairment operator:

$$\widetilde{\phi}_k = \mathcal{D}_\theta(\phi_k)$$

where the impairment parameter set $\theta$ encompasses:

$$\theta = \left\{ \Delta \tau, \, b, \, \tau_r, \, a_3, \, \epsilon_\phi, \, \epsilon_g, \, H_{\mathrm{pkg}}, \, \sigma_j \right\}$$

Table 1. Physical impairment parameters included in the geometry-aware waveform model

Parameter Physical Definition Typical Scaling & Units
$\Delta \tau$ Timing skew between channels Picoseconds ($\text{ps}$) or fraction of chip period
$b$ Phase-quantizer resolution $2$ to $6$ bits
$\tau_r$ Rise/fall time of switching circuits Picoseconds ($\text{ps}$) or slew rate ($\text{V/ns}$)
$a_3$ Third-order non-linear coefficient $\text{V}^{-2}$ (governing $\text{IIP3}$)
$\epsilon_\phi$ Residual phase error Degrees ($^\circ$)
$\epsilon_g$ Static gain mismatch Decibels ($\text{dB}$) or percent ratio
$H_{\mathrm{pkg}}(f)$ Package, bondwire, or interconnect transfer function S-parameters ($\text{S}_{21}$, $\text{S}_{11}$)
$\sigma_j$ Aperture or clock jitter Femtoseconds ($\text{fs}$)

Let $R \in \mathbb{C}^{d \times d}$ be the noise-plus-interference covariance matrix observed at the receiver input. The whitened, normalized realized signature vector for channel $k$ is:

$$u_k = \frac{R^{-1/2}\widetilde{\phi}_k}{\left\| R^{-1/2}\widetilde{\phi}_k \right\|_2}$$

Collect these unit-norm vectors into a signature matrix $U = [u_1, u_2, \ldots, u_N] \in \mathbb{C}^{d \times N}$. The realized hardware Gram matrix is:

$$G = U^H U, \quad \text{where} \quad G_{kj} = u_k^H u_j$$

The mutual coherence of the system is the maximum off-diagonal magnitude:

$$\mu_{\mathrm{real}} = \max_{k \neq j} |G_{kj}|$$

Core Hypothesis Formulation

Finite-alphabet, circuit-realizable waveform signatures can be synthesized such that the realized mutual coherence $\mu_{\mathrm{real}}$ remains near the theoretical Grassmannian or Welch bound under physical impairments $\theta$. This bound-approaching coherence minimizes inter-channel crosstalk, preserves matched-filter SINR, prevents ill-conditioning in digital linear recovery (ZF/MMSE), and relaxes the dynamic-range and ENOB requirements of the digitizer.

3.1 Reviewer-Safe Positioning and Scope Boundaries

To ensure an objective and scientifically rigorous review, the scope of this project is bounded by the following guiding principles:

  • No Magic "Zero-Crosstalk" Claims: Grassmannian geometry guarantees bounded, predictable coherence, not zero crosstalk. The focus is on ensuring crosstalk is deterministic, measurable, and stable.
  • Fully Digital remains the Information-Theoretic Upper Bound: Under infinite power, area, and ADC bandwidth, a fully digital system is always optimal. Our method is a highly efficient alternative when physical space, power, and thermal budgets constrain the architecture.
  • Analog Correlation has Pre-Stage Limitations: Performing analog correlation before the ADC protects the digitizer and subsequent stages from saturation. However, it does not protect the very first active components (e.g., LNAs, mixers) if blockers drive them into compression. The receiver cascade must be modeled using strict non-linear intermodulation equations.
  • Quantum Reach is scoped at the Interface Level: We do not claim to operate a multi-qubit processor. Instead, we develop an interface-level emulator using passive microwave components to demonstrate that our signal separation principles transfer directly to cryogenic readout and selective pulse-shaping environments.

4. Mathematical and Physical Foundations

4.1 Temporal CDM Orthogonality and Its Physical Collapse

Consider $N$ channels multiplexed onto a single shared interface. Let channel $k$ be assigned an ideal normalized temporal code:

$$c_k \in \mathbb{C}^L, \quad \text{with} \quad \|c_k\|_2 = 1$$

The symbol vector is $b = [b_1, \ldots, b_N]^T \in \mathbb{C}^N$ with power scaling $P = \text{diag}(P_1, \ldots, P_N)$. The received signal vector over a block of length $L$ is:

$$y = C P^{1/2} b + n$$

where $C = [c_1, \ldots, c_N] \in \mathbb{C}^{L \times N}$ and $n \sim \mathcal{CN}(0, \sigma^2 I_L)$. Matched-filter detection yields:

$$z = C^H y = C^H C P^{1/2} b + C^H n$$

The temporal code Gram matrix is $G_{\mathrm{code}} = C^H C$, with elements:

$$[G_{\mathrm{code}}]_{ij} = c_i^H c_j$$

In an ideal system, $G_{\mathrm{code}} = I_N$. Under timing skew, however, the codes are offset by a fractional chip delay $\Delta\tau$, shifting the received code matrix to $C(\Delta\tau)$. The resulting leakage from channel $j$ into channel $i$ is:

$$\text{leakage}_{i \leftarrow j} = P_j \left| c_i^H c_j(\Delta\tau) \right|^2$$

This leakage degrades the amplitude and power isolation, which are defined in decibels as:

$$I^{\mathrm{amp}}_{ij} = -20 \log_{10} |c_i^H c_j| \quad \text{and} \quad I^{\mathrm{pow}}_{ij} = -10 \log_{10} |c_i^H c_j|^2$$

4.2 Physical-Mode Signatures and Spatial Packing

To prevent this collapse, we augment the temporal code with a physical signature vector $w_k \in \mathbb{C}^M$, where $M$ is the number of physical paths (such as antenna elements or delay taps). For a four-path physical interface ($M=4$), the signature vector is:

$$w_k = \begin{bmatrix} a_{1k}e^{j\phi_{1k}} \\ a_{2k}e^{j\phi_{2k}} \\ a_{3k}e^{j\phi_{3k}} \\ a_{4k}e^{j\phi_{4k}} \end{bmatrix}$$

This signature is not the transmitted data or the temporal code, but the calibrated physical excitation state across the ports. Collecting these signatures into $W = [w_1, \ldots, w_N] \in \mathbb{C}^{M \times N}$, we define the physical Gram matrix:

$$G_{\mathrm{phys}} = W^H W, \quad [G_{\mathrm{phys}}]_{ij} = w_i^H w_j$$

We formulate the physical phase packing optimization as:

$$\boxed{\begin{aligned} \min_{\{w_k\}} & \max_{i \neq j} \left| w_i^H w_j \right| \\ \text{subject to } & |w_{mk}| \leq a_{\max} \\ & \phi_{mk} \in \mathcal{Q}_b = \left\{ 0, \frac{2\pi}{2^b}, \ldots, \frac{2\pi(2^b-1)}{2^b} \right\} \\ & \text{PAPR}(w_k) \leq \Gamma_{\max} \\ & \text{SFDR} \geq \text{SFDR}_{\min} \\ & P_{\mathrm{IM3}} \leq P_{\mathrm{IM3,max}} \\ & \text{Gain}(w_k) \geq g_{\min} \end{aligned}}$$

For constant-modulus, phase-only physical architectures, we constrain $|w_{mk}| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}$, making the optimization variables the quantized phases $\phi_{mk}$.

4.3 The Product Spherical-Gold Mechanism

By assigning each channel both a temporal code $c_k \in \mathbb{C}^L$ and a physical signature $w_k \in \mathbb{C}^M$, the synthesized joint signature is the tensor product:

$$\phi_k = c_k \otimes w_k \in \mathbb{C}^{LM}$$

Using the tensor product identity, the inner product between two joint signatures is:

$$\phi_i^H \phi_j = (c_i \otimes w_i)^H (c_j \otimes w_j) = (c_i^H c_j)(w_i^H w_j)$$

This leads directly to the Product Law of Mutual Coherence:

$$\boxed{G_{\mathrm{eff}} = G_{\mathrm{code}} \circ G_{\mathrm{phys}}}$$

where $\circ$ denotes the Hadamard (element-wise) product. Under a timing skew impairment $\Delta\tau$, the joint signature's coherence scales as:

$|G_{ij}(\Delta\tau)| = |\rho^{\mathrm{code}}_{ij}(\Delta\tau)| \cdot |\rho^{\mathrm{phys}}_{ij}|$

Expressed in decibels, the total isolation is the sum of the temporal and physical isolations:

$$\boxed{I_{\mathrm{total,dB}}(\Delta\tau) = I_{\mathrm{code,dB}}(\Delta\tau) + I_{\mathrm{phys,dB}}}$$

This relation forms the core of our Spherical-Gold mechanism. If a timing skew causes the temporal code isolation to collapse from $40\,\text{dB}$ to $15\,\text{dB}$, a physical-mode isolation of $15\,\text{dB}$ ensures the system maintains $30\,\text{dB}$ of total isolation.

4.4 Boundary of Validity for the Product Law

The product law assumes that the temporal and physical impairments are mathematically separable:

$$\mathcal{D}_\theta(c_k \otimes w_k) \approx \mathcal{D}_t(c_k) \otimes \mathcal{D}_s(w_k)$$

This separability can fail in physical hardware due to non-linear intermodulation, frequency-dependent group delay, package resonances, and coupling. A key goal of Thrust 1 is to model these non-separable regimes and develop robust receiver kernels to handle them.

5. Capacity, SINR, and Dynamic Range Analysis

5.1 Information Capacity and Gram Conditioning

Under equal power allocation, the multi-channel information capacity is:

$$C = \log_2 \det \left( I_N + \frac{\gamma}{N} G_{\mathrm{real}} \right) = \sum_{\ell=1}^N \log_2 \left( 1 + \frac{\gamma}{N} \lambda_{\ell}(G_{\mathrm{real}}) \right)$$

where $\gamma$ is the average SNR, and $\lambda_\ell$ are the eigenvalues of $G_{\mathrm{real}}$. Using Gershgorin Disc Theorem, the eigenvalues of $G_{\mathrm{real}}$ are bounded by:

$$1 - (N-1)\mu_{\mathrm{real}} \leq \lambda_\ell(G_{\mathrm{real}}) \leq 1 + (N-1)\mu_{\mathrm{real}}$$

which bounds the condition number of the Gram matrix:

$$\kappa(G_{\mathrm{real}}) \leq \frac{1 + (N-1)\mu_{\mathrm{real}}}{1 - (N-1)\mu_{\mathrm{real}}} \quad (\text{for } (N-1)\mu_{\mathrm{real}} < 1)$$

Minimizing the mutual coherence $\mu_{\mathrm{real}}$ prevents the Gram matrix from becoming ill-conditioned, which minimizes noise enhancement during digital signal separation (ZF/MMSE).

5.2 Matched-Filter SINR and High-SNR Dynamic Range Ceiling

For general unequal power channels, the output SINR of channel $i$ under matched-filter detection is:

$$\text{SINR}_i = \frac{P_i |G_{ii}|^2}{\sigma_i^2 + \sum_{j \neq i} P_j |G_{ij}|^2 + P_{\mathrm{IM3}} + P_{\mathrm{jitter}} + P_{\mathrm{quant}}}$$

For equal-power channels ($P_k = P$) and worst-case coherence $\mu_{\mathrm{real}}$, the SINR is bounded by:

$$\text{SINR}_i \geq \frac{\gamma}{1 + \gamma(N-1)\mu_{\mathrm{real}}^2}$$

At high input SNR ($\gamma \to \infty$), the SINR saturates, creating a dynamic-range ceiling:

$$\text{SINR}_{\infty} = \frac{1}{(N-1)\mu_{\mathrm{real}}^2}$$
$$\text{DR}_{\text{ceiling,dB}} = -10 \log_{10} \left[ (N-1) \mu_{\mathrm{real}}^2 \right]$$

Applying our Spherical-Gold physical signature, the mutual coherence becomes $\mu_{\mathrm{real}} = \mu_{\mathrm{code}} \cdot \mu_s$. The resulting dynamic-range improvement over a purely code-multiplexed baseline is:

$$\boxed{\Delta \text{DR} = -20 \log_{10} \mu_s}$$

For a four-channel physical array with optimized constant-modulus signatures yielding $\mu_s = 0.25$, the dynamic range ceiling increases by:

$\Delta \text{DR} = -20 \log_{10}(0.25) = 12.04\,\text{dB}$

This $12\,\text{dB}$ improvement directly relaxes the dynamic-range requirements of subsequent receiver components, such as the active mixers and ADCs.

5.3 Bit Error Rate (BER) Sensitivity

For BPSK modulation, the bit error rate is:

$$P_b = Q\left(\sqrt{2\,\text{SINR}_i}\right) = \frac{1}{2} \text{erfc}\left( \sqrt{\text{SINR}_i} \right)$$

By substituting the impaired coherence into this expression, we map physical hardware non-idealities directly to system-level BER performance.

6. ADC Dynamic Range and ENOB Relief

Let $P_s$ be the desired signal power at the ADC input, and $P_I$ be the power of an in-band blocker or interferer. The blocker-to-signal ratio is defined as:

$B_I = 10 \log_{10} \left( \frac{P_I}{P_s} \right)$

If the receiver provides an analog pre-ADC suppression $A_{\mathrm{pre}}$ (via spatial nulling, code despreading, or filtering), the residual blocker ratio at the converter input is:

$\Gamma_{\mathrm{res}} = 10^{(B_I - A_{\mathrm{pre}})/10}$

The required ADC dynamic range to digitize $J_{\mathrm{shared}}$ signals in the presence of $L_I$ blockers is:

$$\text{DR}_{\text{ADC,dB}} = \text{SNR}_{\text{req,dB}} + M_{\text{head,dB}} + 10 \log_{10} \left[ J_{\text{shared}} + \sum_{\ell=1}^{L_I} 10^{(B_{I,\ell} - A_{\mathrm{pre},\ell})/10} \right]$$

where $M_{\text{head}}$ is the peak-to-average power ratio (PAPR) headroom. The required Effective Number of Bits (ENOB) is:

$$\text{ENOB}_{\text{req}} = \frac{\text{DR}_{\text{ADC,dB}} - 1.76}{6.02}$$

Under Spherical-Gold multiplexing, the analog pre-suppression is the sum of the temporal, spatial, and filtering suppressions:

$A_{\mathrm{pre,SG}} = -20 \log_{10} \left( |\rho_{\mathrm{code}}| \cdot |\rho_{\mathrm{spatial}}| \right) + A_{\mathrm{LPF}}$

This attenuation of out-of-channel interference before digitization lowers the required ADC dynamic range. This translates directly to reduced power consumption, as ADC power scales with sampling rate and resolution:

$$P_{\mathrm{ADC}} \propto N_{\mathrm{ADC}} \cdot f_s \cdot 2^{\mathrm{ENOB}}$$

Reducing the required ENOB by $2$ bits reduces the digitizer's power dissipation by nearly a factor of four.

7. Detailed Research Thrusts

Thrust 1 — Theory: Realized-Gram Geometry for Hardware-Constrained Waveforms

Objective

Establish the theoretical framework and optimization algorithms to map finite-alphabet, circuit-constrained waveforms to the hardware-realized Gram matrix $G_{\mathrm{real}}$ under non-linear, time-varying, and non-separable impairments.

Research Questions

  • RQ1.1: How does the physical impairment operator $\mathcal{D}_\theta$ perturb the off-diagonal coherence and eigenspace of $G_{\mathrm{real}}$?
  • RQ1.2: Can finite-alphabet codebooks keep the realized coherence $\mu_{\mathrm{real}}$ near the Welch bound after phase quantization?
  • RQ1.3: Under what physical conditions does the separability product law $G_{\mathrm{eff}} = G_{\mathrm{code}} \circ G_{\mathrm{phys}}$ fail, and how do we optimize non-separable signatures?

Approach and Methodology

We will model the impairment operator $\mathcal{D}_\theta$ using analytical and computational methods:

  1. Impairment Sensitivity Mapping: Derive analytical bounds for the coherence perturbation $\Delta\mu = |\mu_{\mathrm{real}} - \mu_{\mathrm{ideal}}|$ as a function of phase quantization $b$, timing skew $\Delta\tau$, and third-order non-linearities $a_3$.
  2. Manifold-Constrained Optimization: Develop projection-based algorithms on complex Grassmannian manifolds to search for optimal signature matrices $U$ subject to finite phase states ($b \in \{2, 3, 4\}\,\text{bits}$) and peak-to-average power ratio (PAPR) limits.
  3. Modeling Non-Separable Interconnects: Analyze the breakdown of the product law under non-separable coupling, such as frequency-dependent package resonances or LNA compression. We will develop augmented receiver kernels $K_{\mathrm{rx}}$ to whiten the received signals and restore the conditioning of the Gram matrix.

Deliverables

  • D1.1: Realized-Gram mathematical formalism and bounding proofs.
  • D1.2: Open-source Monte-Carlo simulator mapping hardware impairments to $\mu_{\mathrm{real}}$, $\kappa(G_{\mathrm{real}})$, and capacity.
  • D1.3: Optimization toolkit for synthesizing finite-alphabet Grassmannian codes under hardware constraints.

Thrust 2 — Circuits: Shared-IF Waveform Synthesis and Analog Correlation Before ADC

Objective

Design and implement the integrated circuit blocks that physically synthesize finite-alphabet waveforms and perform analog correlation before the signal reaches the ADC bottleneck.

Circuit-Level Implementation

We will design a four-channel receiver chain to implement geometry-aware waveform synthesis:

  1. Waveform Synthesizer Block: A polar-DAC phase-modulation circuit operating at Ka-band ($26.5\text{--}40\,\text{GHz}$) using a passive switching network to synthesize finite-alphabet phase states ($2\text{--}4\,\text{bits}$) with constant impedance to minimize reflection and group delay variations.
  2. Analog Correlator Block: A wideband active multiplier based on an optimized Gilbert-cell topology. The correlator multiplies the shared-IF signal by the target channel's reference code, performing spatial-code projection in the analog domain.
  3. Low-Pass Filter and Digitizer Interface: A passive, multi-pole low-pass filter integrated with the correlator output to suppress out-of-channel blockers and intermodulation products before digitization, maximizing the dynamic-range relief.

Deliverables

  • D2.1: Schematic, layout, and tape-out files for the Ka-band waveform synthesizer and active correlator.
  • D2.2: Characterization report detailing measured $\text{IIP3}$, $P_{\text{1dB}}$, noise figure, and phase accuracy.
  • D2.3: Verification of the analog correlation block showing pre-ADC suppression of unwanted channels.

Thrust 3 — Platform A: Phased-Array / Multibeam Primary Validation

Objective

Validate the geometry-aware waveform synthesis framework on a physical, hardware-in-the-loop Ka-band phased-array receiver platform.

Experimental Methodology

We will construct a Ka-band ($28\,\text{GHz}$) testbed using a 4-antenna linear array. The testbed will feature:

  1. Multi-Signal Transmission Environment: Integrated signal generators to transmit concurrent target signals and wideband jammers from different spatial angles.
  2. Coherence and Isolation Sweeps: Systematic sweeps of timing skew ($\Delta\tau$), phase quantization ($b$), and temperature-induced drift to measure the isolation profiles of Walsh-CDM, Gold-CDM, and our Spherical-Gold codes.
  3. Spatial Resolution & Cramér-Rao Bound (CRB) Characterization: Quantify the improvement in Angular-of-Arrival (AoA) estimation and spatial resolution. We will evaluate the local spatial CRB:
    $$\text{var}(\theta) \geq \frac{\sigma^2}{2 \sum_t |\alpha_t|^2 \left\| P_{b(\theta)}^{\perp} \frac{\partial b(\theta)}{\partial\theta} \right\|_2^2}$$
    to demonstrate that our Spherical-Gold codes reduce spatial ambiguity and improve angular resolution.

Deliverables

  • D3.1: Phased-array hardware-in-the-loop testing platform operating at $28\,\text{GHz}$.
  • D3.2: Measured isolation and BER curves under controlled timing skew and phase quantization.
  • D3.3: Experimental demonstration of blocker suppression and ADC dynamic-range relief in multi-signal scenarios.

Thrust 4 — Platform B: Qubit-Interface Benchmark and Emulation

Objective

Translate the phase-correlation and Gram-conditioning principles to cryogenic quantum interfaces, validating performance improvements on a high-fidelity quantum interface emulator.

Modeling and Implementation

  1. Readout Signature Optimization: Model an FDM resonator-bank readout interface for superconducting qubits. The reflection signature ($S_{11}$) for channel $k$ is:
    $$S_{11,k}^{(s)}(\omega) \approx 1 - \frac{\kappa_{e,k}}{j[\omega - \omega_{r,k} - (-1)^s \chi_k] + \kappa_k/2}$$
    where $s \in \{0,1\}$ is the qubit state, $\omega_{r,k}$ is the resonator frequency, $\kappa_k$ is the linewidth, and $\chi_k$ is the dispersive shift. The state-separation vector is:
    $$d_k(\omega) = y_k^{(1)}(\omega) - y_k^{(0)}(\omega)$$
    We will optimize the readout pulse spectrum $P_k(\omega)$ using Grassmannian packing to minimize the off-diagonal terms of the readout Gram matrix $G^{\mathrm{ro}}$, reducing readout crosstalk under tight photon budgets and TWPA compression limits.
  2. Crosstalk Characterization: Approximate the readout crosstalk from channel $j$ into channel $i$ as:
    $$\epsilon^{\mathrm{ro}}_{i \leftarrow j} \approx \frac{|d_i^H R^{-1} d_j|^2}{(d_i^H R^{-1} d_i)(d_j^H R^{-1} d_j)} = |G_{ij}^{\mathrm{ro}}|^2$$
    demonstrating that Grassmannian packing of state-separation vectors directly minimizes readout errors.
  3. XY Control Waveform Shaping: Synthesize selective control pulses $p_k(t) = \sum_{\ell=1}^d x_{k,\ell} b_\ell(t)$ to minimize leakage into adjacent qubits. We will optimize the pulse coefficients $x_k$ by solving:
    $$\min_{x_k} \left\| A_{\mathrm{ctrl}}x_k - \Theta_{\mathrm{gate}} e^{j\phi_{g,k}} e_k \right\|_2^2 + \lambda \|x_k\|_2^2 + \gamma \sum_{i \neq k} |[A_{\mathrm{ctrl}}x_k]_i|^2$$
    subject to DAC bandwidth, amplitude, and gate-duration constraints. This ensures precise gate angles ($\Theta_k = \int_0^{T_g} \Omega_k(t) dt$) while minimizing cross-talk.

Deliverables

  • D4.1: Multi-channel FDM resonator-bank hardware emulator.
  • D4.2: Software package for optimizing selective XY control pulses and readout signatures.
  • D4.3: Measured crosstalk, assignment fidelity, and power consumption benchmarks comparing Grassmannian-optimized signatures to standard FDM readouts.

9. Falsification Framework and Failure Modes

To maintain scientific integrity and accelerate development, the project will actively test the limits and failure modes of the proposed technology. We have identified nine potential failure modes along with systematic testing procedures:

FM1: Non-Separable Interconnect Dispersion

Risk: The product law $G_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx G_{\mathrm{code}} \circ G_{\mathrm{phys}}$ breaks down under severe, frequency-dependent package dispersion.

Test: Introduce highly dispersive passive microstrip networks between the antenna elements and the mixing stages, and measure the degradation of the product isolation law.

FM2: RF Pre-Stage Compression

Risk: Large out-of-channel blockers compress the LNA or mixer before the analog correlator can apply suppression.

Test: Sweep the power of an out-of-channel blocker from $-40\,\text{dBm}$ to $0\,\text{dBm}$ and measure the input $1\,\text{dB}$ compression point ($P_{1\mathrm{dB}}$) to map the receiver's physical limits.

FM3: Calibration Null Collapse

Risk: Phase and gain drift over temperature and time degrade the optimized physical signatures, collapsing the nulls.

Test: Operate the hardware platform inside a temperature-controlled chamber, sweep the temperature from $0^\circ\text{C}$ to $85^\circ\text{C}$, and track the drift of the mutual coherence matrix $G_{\mathrm{real}}(t, T)$.

FM4: Walsh Code Timing Collapse

Risk: Fractional-chip timing offsets completely destroy the orthogonality of Walsh-Hadamard codes.

Test: Sweep the inter-channel delay offset $\Delta\tau$ from $0$ to $1$ chip period, and plot the isolation profile to compare the stability of Walsh-CDM against Spherical-Gold coding.

FM5: Gold Code Length and Bandwidth Limitations

Risk: Achieving low mutual coherence with temporal codes requires long sequence lengths, which increases bandwidth and power consumption.

Test: Parameterize the temporal code length $L \in \{7, 15, 31, 63, 127\}$, and measure the tradeoff between code length, system bandwidth, and switching power dissipation.

FM6: ADC Saturation by Shared-IF Signals

Risk: The summation of multiple shared-IF signals saturates the ADC full-scale range, erasing the expected ENOB relief.

Test: Increase the number of concurrent channels from $2$ to $8$ and measure the ADC clipping rates and resulting intermodulation distortion.

FM7: Qubit Readout Photon-Starvation

Risk: Restricting readout power to avoid driving resonators into non-linear regimes starves the receiver of photons, degrading the measurement SNR.

Test: Sweep the average cavity photon number $\bar{n} = 10^{-2}$ to $10^2$ and plot the assignment fidelity as a function of integration time and photon budget.

FM8: Pulse Duration and Peak Power Tradeoffs

Risk: Enforcing spectral nulls on XY control pulses increases their duration or PAPR, exceeding the qubit's coherence time or driving the DAC into saturation.

Test: Synthesize selective pulses under strict spectral leakage constraints, and measure the resulting pulse duration and PAPR to define the operational limits of the transmitter.

FM9: Calibration-Limited Zero-Forcing Recovery

Risk: Ill-conditioning of the Gram matrix under drift amplifies noise during digital linear recovery (ZF/MMSE), rendering the system calibration-limited.

Test: Introduce intentional calibration errors into the digital receiver kernel and measure the resulting degradation in BER and throughput.

11. Broader Impacts and Integrated Educational Plan

This CAREER project integrates cutting-edge research with an active, multi-level educational plan designed to train the next generation of engineers in cross-disciplinary circuit-signal co-design.

11.1 New Graduate Module Development

We will develop a new graduate-level module: Realized-Gram Methods for RF and Quantum Interfaces. The course will bridge the gap between physical electromagnetics and digital signal processing, covering:

  • Grassmannian manifold optimization.
  • The physics of non-linear distortion, phase noise, and timing jitter in multiplexed systems.
  • Digital and analog calibration techniques for restoring Gram matrix conditioning.

11.2 Undergraduate Laboratory Kits and Modules

We will introduce hands-on laboratory modules into the core undergraduate RF/Microwave and Communications courses. Students will use low-cost software-defined radios (SDRs) and custom PCB correlators to:

  • Transmit and decode Walsh-coded signals.
  • Observe firsthand how timing skew degrades signal isolation.
  • Implement simple phase-coding schemes to restore signal separation, bringing theoretical concepts to life.

11.3 Open-Source Visualization Toolkit

We will develop an interactive, web-based simulation tool using the Gemini API and modern web frameworks. The tool will allow students, educators, and industry professionals to:

  • Set up arbitrary multiplexing codebooks (TDM, FDM, CDM, Spherical-Gold).
  • Introduce custom hardware impairments (such as timing jitter or non-linearities) using slider controls.
  • Instantly visualize the resulting degradation of the Gram matrix, constellation diagrams, and bit error rates.

11.4 Outreach and Diversity Initiatives

We will partner with our institution's diversity in engineering programs to recruit and support underrepresented students:

  • High School Engineering Camps: Host interactive workshops demonstrating the physics of wave interference and coding using acoustic wave transmitters and simple receiver apps.
  • Undergraduate Research Fellowships: Provide fully funded summer research opportunities in our laboratory, actively recruiting female and underrepresented minority students to build a diverse pipeline for advanced graduate studies.

12. Conclusion

This CAREER proposal presents a unified, scientifically rigorous, and physically grounded approach to resolving the shared-interface bottleneck in next-generation communication and quantum systems. By elevating the hardware-realized Gram matrix to a primary object of co-design, this project departs from traditional "ideal-orthogonality" assumptions, establishing a new paradigm of geometry-aware waveform synthesis.

Validated first on a high-frequency phased-array receiver platform and then translated to a cryogenic quantum interface emulator, the research is tightly coupled with an educational plan designed to inspire, educate, and diversify the engineering talent pool. Through rigorous testing, active exploration of failure modes, and open-source software release, this project will deliver both foundational scientific insights and practical design tools, paving the way for scalable, energy-efficient RF and quantum interfaces.
